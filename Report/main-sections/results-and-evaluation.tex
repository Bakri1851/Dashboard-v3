This chapter evaluates the dashboard system of Chapter~\ref{sec:implementation} along two dimensions, the validity of the model outputs (whether the struggle and difficulty rankings agree with observable student behaviour) and the operational behaviour of the deployed software against the responsiveness, interpretability, and robustness targets implied in Chapter~\ref{sec:requirements}. 
The unit of evaluation is the individual weekly lab sessions as the dashboard is designed to be used within a single session and the cohort consists of approximately 140 students. The evaluation focuses on the module from which data was collected, \textbf{25COA122}, across 23 recorded lab sessions from 2 February 2026 to 15 May 2026.
On most weeks there were two lab sessions running back-to-back, each lasting an hour, except for the first and last week which had one session each.

\subsection{Evaluation Design} \label{sec:eval-design}

\subsubsection{Scope of Evaluation}
The evaluation is a retrospective replay of the submission logs already collected during the deployment, assessing each model output at varying cutoff points \(T\) against ground-truth labels derived from behaviour in the same logs, which lets us examine the dashboard's prioritisation as if it were running live without conducting a fresh classroom experiment.
We additionally evaluate the v2 weight vectors that were refitted against second-opinion labels obtained from an LLM rater.
A prospective intervention study, which would test whether dashboard-informed instructor decisions improve student outcomes, is explicitly out of scope; the deployment window did not permit a controlled comparison, the anonymisation of student identifiers rules out grade linkage, and submission records cannot currently be mapped to individual lab seats, so any causal interpretation is left to future work.

The functional and non-functional testing is also carried out through smoke-testing and end-to-end testing of the deployed V2 stack, where we record evidence of the system's behaviour and performance against the targets set in Chapter~\ref{sec:requirements}.
We will present a qualitative summary in the main body with the full row-by-row checklist appearing in Appendix~\ref{app:detailed-test-results}. We do not run a load test, a security audit, or an accessibility audit.

\subsubsection{Evidence Sources}

The evaluation draws on five sources of evidence. 
The primary source is the deployment submission log, a newline delimited JSON record exported from the platform's submission API and parsed by the data loader of Section~\ref{sec:analytics} into a normalised DataFrame indexed by student, question, and timestamp. 
The log spans the academic period of deployment and contains the per-submission fields the model pipeline relies on, namely the question attempted, module label, submitted answers, AI feedback and timestamp; these fields feed the derived signals recomputed at replay time.

The second source is the second-opinion label set obtained from a large language model rater (GPT-4o), which assigns each of the $1{,}306$ stratified snapshots across $140$ distinct users a struggle rating from the four defined bands, and each of the $72$ unique questions a difficulty rating from the four defined bands.
The label set is important for the training of the v2 model and its agreement against the $50$ human calibration set is reported in Section~\ref{sec:eval-tradeoffs} and also bounds the interpretive strength of the v2 numbers.

The third source is the survey of Section~\ref{sec:eval-survey}, which is an eleven-question Microsoft Forms survey distributed to undergraduate students, PhD student lab assistants and staff who are involved in computer lab sessions.
The survey provides Likert and free-text evidence on baseline lab support, dashboard interpretability, trust, and ethical concerns. 

The fourth source is the smoke testing and end-to-end testing of the deployed V2 stack, where each of the functional and non-functional requirements defined in Chapter~\ref{sec:requirements} is exercised manually and the observed behaviour recorded; the resulting checklist appears in Appendix~\ref{app:detailed-test-results}, and the body of this chapter (Section~\ref{sec:eval-functional}) records only the qualitative summary and the small number of items that failed or required workaround.

The fifth source is the V2 prewarm log, which records the wall-clock cost of each startup phase (incorrectness cache warm, IRT fit, BKT fit, struggle leaderboard, difficulty leaderboard) together with per-skill BKT predictive area under the curve (AUC) and which underpins the latency and per-skill BKT evidence reported in Section~\ref{sec:eval-results}.



\subsubsection{V2 Training Methodology} \label{sec:eval-v2-methodology}

The deployed system is the trained v2 model, and this section evaluates it. The hand-set v1 weights are the starting point it evolved from rather than a competing model evaluated in its own right.
The v2 model fits the weights against second-opinion labels obtained from a large language model rater, and the resulting v2 weights are evaluated under the same evaluation framework.
Of the models introduced in Chapter~\ref{sec:implementation}, three composite weight vectors and two scalar hyperparameters are re-tuned.
The per-skill BKT parameters and the 2PL IRT parameters are retained from the fitting documented in Chapter~\ref{sec:implementation} and are not re-trained here.
The mistake-clustering pipeline, the measurement-confidence formula, and the incorrectness scorer carry no learnable weights so they are excluded from the training. 
For the evaluation we have training data which are 1{,}306 stratified snapshots across twenty-one healthy sessions for the struggle model and the improved model, and seventy-two unique questions for the difficulty model, all labelled by GPT-4o. 
The agreement between the LLM rater and a fifty-snapshot author calibration is reported in Section~\ref{sec:eval-tradeoffs}.


\subsubsection{Limitations of Evaluation}
Several limitations of the evaluation are worth noting, all of which follow from the evaluation context rather than the evaluation design. 
The first is the small effective cohort, with the v2 model training drawing on 1{,}306 stratified snapshots across twenty-one healthy sessions and 140 unique students, for the struggle model and the improved model, and 72 unique questions for the difficulty model.

The second is the single module, single institution context which limits the generalisability of the findings. 
Behaviour of the dashboard on a different module or different form of submission is not directly inferable from the results presented here, and we report findings only in the context of the 25COA122 module and the submission format of Loughborough University Computer Science lab modules. 

The third is the retrospective nature of the evaluation, which is necessary for the context of this report but still leaves a gap about whether instructor decisions actually informed by the dashboard improves student outcomes. Results focus on agreement with the LLM rater and not the causal impact on student outcomes, and any causal interpretation is left to a prospective study outside the scope of this report.


\subsection{Functional Testing} \label{sec:eval-functional}

\subsubsection{Data Ingestion and Session Handling}
The first functional requirement of Chapter~\ref{sec:requirements} (FR1, live submission-data ingestion) was validated during the V2 deployment against a live session, confirming that the JSON log returned by the Loughborough submission API is parsed correctly end-to-end into the normalised submissions DataFrame, that the XML field embedded in each record is extracted into per-submission attributes, and that the in-memory cache invalidates correctly under the configured TTL described in Section~\ref{sec:pipeline}.
Session start and end were validated through the lab instructor dashboard and the lab assistant join flow concurrently, confirming that the shared state file at \texttt{data/lab\_session.json} updates correctly under the \texttt{filelock} pattern described in Section~\ref{sec:shared-state}.
No data loss or corruption was found across the test runs. The full FR1 verification row appears in Appendix~\ref{app:detailed-test-results}.

\subsubsection{Analytics and Model Behaviour}
Functional requirements FR2 (computation of student struggle) and FR3 (computation of question difficulty) of Chapter~\ref{sec:requirements} were validated against the analytical pipeline of Chapter~\ref{sec:implementation}.
The seven-signal baseline struggle model and the five-signal baseline difficulty model were confirmed to produce scores in $[0, 1]$ across the cohort, with the four band-classification labels classifying correctly under both the v1 and v2 weight regimes.
The collaborative-filtering layer, the mistake-clustering pipeline, the IRT 2PL fit, the BKT mastery estimates, and the improved-struggle model were tested end-to-end and the outputs were cross-checked against the design equations of Chapter~\ref{sec:design}.
The graceful weight-redistribution cases described in Section~\ref{sec: improved struggle} (BKT unavailable, IRT unavailable, both unavailable) were each tested with mock data and confirmed to redistribute the weights as expected. No silent failures were observed. The per-model verification rows appear in Appendix~\ref{app:detailed-test-results}.

\subsubsection{Instructor Dashboard Features}
FR4 (real-time prioritisation) and FR5 (present relevant analytics) of Chapter~\ref{sec:requirements} were validated through the V2 FastAPI + React stack.
Each of the seven instructor views was exercised to confirm the rendered components matched the design specification: the In-Class leaderboards are ordered by score under both the Basic and Advanced modes, the band colour coding tracks the threshold classification map of Section~\ref{sec: student struggle}, and the Student Detail, Question Detail, Data Analysis, Session Progression, Previous Sessions, and Settings views render without errors.
Navigation between views was tested and confirmed to be smooth and error-free, and the data displayed in each view was cross-checked against the expected output from the analytics pipeline.
The filtering of the system was also tested to confirm that the per-module and per-session filters correctly update the displayed data and that the system handles edge cases gracefully.
Screenshots for each view appear in Appendix~\ref{app:ui-screenshots}; the full FR4 and FR5 verification rows are in Appendix~\ref{app:detailed-test-results}.

\subsubsection{Lab Assistant Workflow}
FR7 (lab assistant ranking system) of Chapter~\ref{sec:requirements} was validated through the V2 lab-assistant single-page application.
The join flow was tested with the six-character session code described in Section~\ref{sec:assistant}, confirming that the assistant's identity persists across reloads through the \texttt{?aid=} URL parameter and that the polling cycle picks up new assignments without manual refresh.
The mark-helped round-trip was validated, confirming that a button press on the assistant app updates \texttt{data/lab\_session.json} through the same file-locked pattern as the instructor process and that the instructor dashboard sidebar picks up new assistants and their status correctly.
Assistants were also confirmed to be able to join simultaneously without loss of data.
FR6 (smart device) was scoped out at design time due to focusing on the modelling; it remains future work and is restated in Section~\ref{sec:eval-discussion}. The full FR7 verification row appears in Appendix~\ref{app:detailed-test-results}.


\subsection{Non-Functional Testing} \label{sec:eval-nonfunctional}

\subsubsection{Performance and Refresh Behaviour}
NFR1 (Real Time Performance) of Chapter~\ref{sec:requirements} is met through a lifespan-prewarm strategy of Chapter~\ref{sec:implementation}, which pre-computes all derived signals before the FastAPI process accepts requests so that the first user request sees the steady-state latency of the dashboard rather than the startup latency.
The prewarm is made up of seven stages, captured across a representative session of $42{,}443$ submission records across $3{,}935$ unique feedback strings.

The submission log loads and parses in $8.0$~s. The OpenAI incorrectness scoring pass runs in $464.5$~s on a cold start with an empty cache, batching $197$ calls for the $3{,}935$ unique feedback strings, but reduces to a sub-second lookup on subsequent runs through the disk-persisted cache at \texttt{data/incorrectness\_cache.json}; this $71\%$ cold-start cost only occurs once per corpus and never appears in the steady state afterwards.
The BKT parameter fit across the six skills present in the cohort runs in $23.2$~s, fitting three skills and falling back to \texttt{config.py} defaults on three under the gating logic of Section~\ref{sec: bkt}. 
The IRT 2PL fit completes in $7.3$~s on the final $127 \times 86$ response matrix produced by the iterative min-attempts and separable-response drop-pass of Section~\ref{sec: irt}. 
The struggle leaderboard step completes in $6.2$~s and the difficulty leaderboard step in $3.6$~s. The RAG collection takes $137.2$~s to load the sentence-transformers \texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2} model and embed $3{,}069$ sampled rows into the in-memory vector index.

Total prewarm for this session is $649.9$~s on the cold start and approximately $185$~s on a warm start once the incorrectness cache is populated; the RAG embedding is the largest contributor to the prewarm time.
Once the prewarm completes, all subsequent requests respond from in-memory state and the steady-state five-second polling cycle of the lab-assistant app was confirmed to be responsive without observable latency under steady-state operation.
The full NFR1 verification row appears in Appendix~\ref{app:detailed-test-results}.

\subsubsection{Usability and Interpretability}

NFR2 (Interpretability) of Chapter~\ref{sec:requirements} is supported through the design of the instructor dashboard and the survey of Section~\ref{sec:eval-survey}, which evaluated the clarity of the underlying struggle and difficulty scores as well as the intuitiveness of the colour-coded band labels surfaced in the leaderboards. 
On the five-point clarity scale of what each metric means (Q5 of the survey), seven of the ten respondents rated both the struggle and difficulty scores as "Completely Intuitive" and a further two rated them as "Slightly Intuitive"; the same single respondent placed both metrics in the "Slightly Unintuitive" band and no respondent picked either "Neutral" or "Completely Unintuitive" on either metric, yielding a strongly bimodal distribution where disagreement collapses to a single outlier.
On the five-point intuitiveness scale for the colour-coded bands (Q6), the same bimodal pattern held with six respondents at "Completely Intuitive" and three at "Slightly Intuitive" on both band sub-rows, with the same outlier at "Slightly Unintuitive".
The trust question (Q7) returned two respondents at "Very Likely" and five at "Somewhat Likely" to trust the dashboard's prioritisation, one respondent at "Neither Likely nor Unlikely", and two participants on the negative end of the scale (one at "Somewhat Unlikely" and one at "Very Unlikely"); this yielded a $70/10/20$ split between positive, neutral, and negative trust.
The question on what the main issue is when providing real-time support (Q4) had three main themes: not enough staff, quieter students may not be willing to ask for help, and identifying who needs the most help is difficult, all three of which the dashboard's prioritisation features directly target.
NFR2 is met for the surveyed cohort within the limitations of the sample size; the full NFR2 verification row appears in Appendix~\ref{app:detailed-test-results}.

\subsubsection{Robustness and Failure Handling}
NFR3 (Robustness to noisy data) of Chapter~\ref{sec:requirements} is validated through several edge cases being exercised against the deployed V2 stack.
The cold start case where there are no submissions at the session start was tested by joining the dashboard before the prewarm had finished, confirming that the leaderboards render with skeleton loaders and the scoring pipeline returns gracefully without raising exceptions or errors; the IRT fit refuses to fit when fewer than two attempts per question are available and returns empty difficulty, discrimination and ability dictionaries; later stages detect the empty fit through a non-empty check and redistribute the IRT weights to the behavioural part of the improved model under the graceful degradation logic of Section~\ref{sec: improved struggle}.
The single student case (one student active) was tested by replaying a session at the first cutoff; the collaborative filtering layer requires at least $k+1$ active students to compute a similarity ranking and gracefully returns the parametric ranking unchanged when the cohort is too small.
The BKT identifiability case (a session where all attempts on a skill share the same outcome leading to MLE degeneracy) is handled by the gating logic of Section~\ref{sec: bkt} where, if fewer than fifty attempts are recorded or when all attempts are identical, the fitter refuses to fit and the configured defaults are retained; this fallback path was observed in production during the deployment, with the 25COA122 BKT fit returning a scipy \texttt{ABNORMAL\_TERMINATION\_IN\_LNSRCH} status and the gating logic correctly retaining the \texttt{config.py} defaults.
The improved model's weight redistribution behaviour, when BKT mastery or IRT difficulty is unavailable, is implemented as explicit branches in the model code covering the four cases of Section~\ref{sec: improved struggle}; the BKT-unavailable case (case 2) was observed in production via the 25COA122 fallback above, with the missing mastery weight $w_M$ redistributed to the behavioural composite weight $w_B$ as the documented redistribution rule prescribes.
The lab-state file at \texttt{data/lab\_session.json} is protected against concurrent writes through the \texttt{filelock} library, which serialises access under an exclusive lock so that the instructor app, the lab-assistant app, and the FastAPI backend cannot corrupt the shared state when they update it within a polling cycle.
The RAG layer offloads its blocking calls through \texttt{asyncio.to\_thread}, which dispatches the embedding and retrieval work onto a worker thread so the FastAPI event loop remains free to service other requests during a RAG call.
NFR4 (Scalability), NFR5 (Privacy and Ethical), and NFR6 (Extensibility and Maintainability) hold by construction rather than by edge-case testing, and are documented in the NFR table of Appendix~\ref{app:detailed-test-results}.

\subsection{Results} \label{sec:eval-results}

\subsubsection{Cohort Characterisation}

It is useful to characterise what the deployed v2 model produces on the evaluation cohort before the model comparisons.
Figure~\ref{fig:eval-cohort-distributions} shows the v2 struggle bands across the 1{,}306 snapshots, the v2 difficulty bands across the 72 unique questions, and the band assignments the LLM rater produced on the same data.

The cohort skews towards the severe end on both axes.
The deployed model places 40\% of snapshots in "Needs Help" and 31\% in "On Track", which tracks the LLM rater reasonably as it rates 47\% "Needs Help" and only 6\% "On Track".
Difficulty is more skewed , with the model rating 67\% of questions "Very Hard" against the rater's 72\%, and neither placing a meaningful share in the lowest band.
The model is therefore strict for this module rather than the dashboard over-flagging.

Despite being trained on a large language model rater rather than on human marks, the model matches what it was trained on, which is encouraging: the same supervised approach trained on human-rated data would likely bring the model closer to human judgement and yield a more accurate model that matches a human marker's ratings.
Collecting enough human-rated snapshots is infeasible within the constraints of this project; the LLM rater stands in as a scalable second opinion, and its agreement with a fifty-snapshot human calibration is reported in Section~\ref{sec:eval-tradeoffs}.

\subsubsection{Struggle Model Outputs and Quality}

The deployed struggle model is a seven-signal weighted composite fitted by ordinary least-squares against the LLM's four-band rating. Figure~\ref{fig:eval-weights-struggle} shows the trained signed weights. Recent incorrectness carries the largest weight at $+0.31$, followed by the retry rate and mean incorrectness; active time and answer-repetition take small negative weights, which is the fit's way of saying that, once the incorrectness signals are accounted for, a student spending longer or repeating an answer is not independently more likely to be struggling on this cohort.

The weights are stable across the cross-validation folds. Figure~\ref{fig:eval-weights-heatmap} shows the signed-weight heatmap across the five session-grouped folds, where the signs agree row by row and the magnitudes vary little, so the negative weights are reproducible rather than an artefact of a single split. Figure~\ref{fig:eval-rho-perfold-struggle} reports the per-fold held-out Spearman~$\rho$, with a mean of $+0.588$ and a $95\%$ interval of $[+0.490, +0.686]$.

Figure~\ref{fig:eval-ols-diagnostic} plots predicted band severity against the observed LLM band; the predictions track the identity line with a mean absolute error of $0.56$ bands. Training the weights against the rater lifts rank agreement over the original hand-set starting point, from $\rho = +0.471$ to $+0.588$, which is the gain the supervised refit buys.

\subsubsection{Model Comparison} \label{sec:eval-comparison}

Having established the deployed struggle model, we ask whether ordinary least-squares is the right model class for it. Figure~\ref{fig:eval-model-class-bakeoff} compares OLS against regularised linear regressions (Ridge, Lasso, ElasticNet) and two non-linear ensembles (random forest and gradient boosting), each fitted under the same cross-validation against the four-band rating. On struggle, OLS gives the best rank agreement at $\rho = +0.588$ and the linear variants sit within $0.003$ of it, while the ensembles trail. On difficulty and improved-struggle the random forest edges ahead ($+0.479$ against $+0.468$, and $+0.250$ against $+0.201$), but by margins inside the per-fold variation.

We retain OLS rather than the forest for three reasons. Its advantage is small relative to the per-fold spread, and the difficulty fit rests on only seventy-two questions under leave-one-out where the forest tends to overfit; on struggle the forest is clearly behind; and the forest yields no interpretable signed weights, so the sign structure read off in the previous section would be lost. OLS is therefore competitive or better within noise on every target, decisively better on struggle, and the only competitive class that keeps interpretable weights.

A second question is whether to treat the band as an ordinal regression target or to classify it directly. Figure~\ref{fig:eval-framing} compares OLS against three classifiers on ranking (Spearman~$\rho$) and band agreement (linear-weighted~$\kappa$). OLS is competitive on ranking, but the classifiers obtain a higher raw $\kappa$ because they optimise the categorical objective directly; the trained band thresholds recover this for the regression without sacrificing its ranking or interpretability.

Finally, the dashboard offers an improved struggle model that augments the behavioural composite with BKT mastery and IRT difficulty. Figure~\ref{fig:eval-comparison-struggle-scatter} and Table~\ref{tab:eval-comparison-struggle-top10} compare it with the baseline composite: the two agree on the broad rank order ($\rho = +0.496$) but share only four of their top ten, and the improved score compresses the upper tail, so the most-at-risk leaderboard differs. Given its weak rank agreement with the rater ($\rho = +0.201$), the baseline composite is the deployed default and the improved model is retained as a toggle.

\subsubsection{Band-threshold optimisation} \label{sec:eval-thresholds}

Along with the score the dashboard also shows the four-band label, derived by classifying the score with three thresholds.
The thresholds in Sections~\ref{sec:thresholds-struggle} and~\ref{sec:thresholds-difficulty} were hand-set for interpretability, so we had tested to see whether trained thresholds raise band agreement with the rater without altering the underlying score. This subsection reports the answer across six model variants: hand-set composite, trained OLS composite, and improved BKT$+$IRT on the struggle side; hand-set composite, trained OLS composite, and 2PL IRT on the difficulty side.

The three thresholds for each model were tuned by brute-force grid search, maximising linear-weighted Cohen's $\kappa$ against the LLM's four-band rating. 
The grid spanned each cutpoint at $0.005$ resolution over the score range, with a monotonicity constraint $t_1 < t_2 < t_3$ and a minimum band width of $0.1$ to keep every band reachable; a percentile floor on $t_1$ stopped the optimiser collapsing the lowest band on cohorts where most scores cluster near zero. 
Cross-validation used GroupKFold(5) keyed by \texttt{session\_id} for struggle ($n = 1{,}306$ snapshots) and leave-one-out for difficulty ($n = 72$ questions); the reported $\kappa$ is the pooled out-of-fold prediction. The constrained variant is the reported default; the unconstrained variant was run as an upper-bound sanity check and is not reported here.

% --- promotion rule paragraph
The promotion rule was pre-registered before the search: a model variant was promoted to the trained thresholds in \texttt{config.py} only if the constrained CV $\Delta\kappa$ exceeded $+0.05$; otherwise the hand-set thresholds were retained. Four of the six variants cleared the bar (struggle baseline, struggle deployed, difficulty baseline, difficulty deployed); the improved struggle model did not gain (Section~\ref{sec:eval-comparison}) and the IRT difficulty model lost ground (Section~\ref{sec:eval-irt}), so both kept their hand-set thresholds. Per-fold $\kappa$ values for the four promoted rows sat within $\pm 0.03$ of the pooled mean, indicating the lift is not driven by a single fold.

% --- deployed cutpoints paragraph
The struggle deployed and difficulty deployed rows in Table~\ref{tab:eval-thresholds} report training-time agreement on the StandardScaler $[0,3]$ predictor used during the OLS fit. The deployed scorer keeps the min-max $[0,1]$ composite form (Section~\ref{sec:thresholds-struggle} and~\ref{sec:thresholds-difficulty}); rank correlation between the two representations is $+0.994$ (Spearman) so $\rho$ is unchanged, but band cutpoints are scale-dependent and must be re-expressed. A second pass of the same constrained grid search on the deployed min-max composite produced the cutpoints now in \texttt{config.py}: $(0.102, 0.203, 0.326)$ for struggle and $(0.288, 0.388, 0.531)$ for difficulty, with constrained CV $\kappa = +0.384$ (struggle) and $+0.387$ (difficulty).

% --- cohort caveat paragraph
The trained cutpoints reflect the cohort distribution they were fit on, which is heavily skewed toward Needs Help on struggle (the LLM rater placed $47\%$ of snapshots there) and Very Hard on difficulty ($72\%$ of questions). A different cohort - a more homogeneous group, an easier paper, a different module - would shift the score distribution and the optimal cutpoints with it; the threshold-training pass should be re-run rather than the existing tuples reused. The continuous score is more cohort-portable than the bands.


\subsubsection{Hyperparameter optimisation} \label{sec:eval-hyperopt}

Two scalar hyperparameters were tuned using Optuna's Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) \cite{NIPS2011_86e8f7ab, akibaOptunaNextgenerationHyperparameter2019}, maximising session-grouped five-fold cross-validation Spearman~$\rho$ against the LLM's four-band rating. 
The Bayesian shrinkage constant $K$ was tuned in a 50-trial study over integers $[1,20]$ and the collaborative-filtering threshold $\tau$ in a separate 50-trial study over reals in $[0.4, 0.9]$. 
TPE was used over grid and random search due to being significantly better for a small sample size. The shrinkage constant $K$ moves from the initial hand-set default $5$ to the tuned best $0$, with CV $\rho$ changing from $+0.471$ to $+0.481$; $\Delta\rho \approx +0.009$ within the per-fold standard deviation, so the result is a robustness validation of the hand-set value rather than an empirical refinement.
The threshold $\tau$ moves from the initial hand-set default $0.7$ to the tuned best $0.900$, with CV $\rho$ moving from $+0.306$ to $+0.466$; $\Delta\rho \approx +0.160$, which is a substantial improvement indicating that the initial hand-set default was too permissive.
The best $\tau$ sits at the upper boundary of the $[0.4, 0.9]$ search range so a finer search may yield further improvement. 


To validate the independence assumption that the two hyperparameters can be tuned separately a single joint $K \times \tau$ study was run over 100 trials with the joint objective $0.5\,(\rho_K + \rho_\tau)$.  
The joint optimum lands at $K = 1$ and $\tau = 0.898$, coinciding with the independent optima to within the third decimal place, suggesting that the two hyper parameters are decoupled under the objective. 
Figure~\ref{fig:eval-hyperparams-contour} shows the joint optimisation surface, where the ridge of high $\rho$ tracks $\tau$ near the upper boundary of the search range and the surface is essentially flat along $K$, visually confirming the independence. 
Figure~\ref{fig:eval-hyperparams-importances} quantifies this directly as a parameter importance bar chart $\tau$ accounts for the bulk of the joint-objective variance whereas $K$ contributes negligibly. Table~\ref{tab:eval-hyperparams} reports the headline numbers.

\subsection{Survey} \label{sec:eval-survey}

\subsubsection{Instrument, Audience and Ethical Approval}

The survey had been presented as an eleven question Microsoft Forms questionnaire, targeted at undergraduate students, postgraduate lab assistants, and staff involved in the 25COA122 computer-lab sessions. Ten responses were returned: six undergraduate students, one postgraduate-taught student, two postgraduate-research lab assistants, and one staff member. Questions focused on validating our requirements,  free text questions to get opinions on areas related to the dashboard, ethical concerns and feature requests. 
Participation was voluntary, anonymous, and the form recorded no personal identifiers. 
The small sample size limits the survey to a qualitative evaluation against the quantitative results of Section~\ref{sec:eval-results} rather than a statistical claim in its own right.

\subsubsection{Baseline Perception of Lab Support}

Q2 asked whether students who need help during a lab session currently receive it in time. Responses split roughly evenly between the two middle bands: $50\%$ of respondents answered "Always" or "Often" and $40\%$ answered "Sometimes", with one respondent answering "Rarely" and none answering "Never" (Figure~\ref{fig:eval-survey-baseline}). The distribution suggests that labs already work well for half the cohort and is intermittent for the rest, which is the regime the dashboard is designed to act on.

Q3 asked the same respondents how easy it is to identify which students are struggling in a computer lab. The pattern inverts Q2: $60\%$ answered "Somewhat not easy" or "Extremely not easy", and only $20\%$ answered "Somewhat easy" (none answered "Extremely easy"). Lab staff and assistants can describe the issue but cannot reliably see who is needs help, this is the problem the struggle leaderboard is designed to solve.

Q4 asked respondents to describe in free text what they see as the biggest barrier to providing timely support. Nine of ten respondents gave a substantive answer; keyword-coding produced three recurring themes (Table~\ref{tab:eval-survey-themes}): staff shortage (four responses), difficulty identifying which students need help (three), and student reluctance to ask for help unprompted (three). The first two themes are within the dashboard's scope; a prioritised struggle leaderboard reduces the identification load on a thin staffing complement, while help-seeking reluctance is a behavioural barrier that the dashboard mitigates indirectly by surfacing quiet strugglers who would not otherwise raise their hand.

\subsubsection{Student Comfort and Ethical Concerns}

Q9 asked the respondent to imagine being the student whose struggle score is surfaced to the instructor and to rate how comfortable they would be with that. The split is $60/10/30$ comfortable/neutral/uncomfortable (Figure~\ref{fig:eval-survey-comfort}): three respondents answered "Very comfortable", three "Somewhat comfortable", one "Neither", and three "Somewhat uncomfortable". Notably, no respondent answered "Very uncomfortable", so the discomfort that exists is moderate rather than categorical, but a three-in-ten unease rate is large enough that the consent and transparency themes raised in Q10 (below) need treating as live design constraints rather than secondary considerations.

Q8 asked what concerns the respondent would have about using the dashboard in a real lab. Five respondents gave substantive answers; keyword-coding produced two themes (Table~\ref{tab:eval-survey-themes}): surveillance discomfort (three responses, capturing concerns about students feeling monitored, tracked, or under pressure when each submission is logged), and quantifiability limits (two responses, capturing the view that complex student dynamics resist reduction to a single score). The first theme is a deployment concern and is mitigated by the dashboard never being surfaced to students themselves; the second is a model-validity concern that this chapter answers empirically (Section~\ref{sec:eval-results}) by reporting the score's agreement with both the LLM rater and the human calibration set.

Q10 asked specifically for ethical, privacy, or fairness concerns the respondent would want addressed before adoption. Five respondents gave substantive answers, splitting across four themes (Table~\ref{tab:eval-survey-themes}): name visibility and re-identification (two responses, both noting that even an anonymised student ID still lets the lecturer recognise the same struggler week after week), consent and transparency (one response, on informing students before deployment), lecturer bias on grading (one response, on the risk of dashboard-driven impressions leaking into coursework marking), and data-use scope (one response, asking what wider university data the score might be combined with). Three of the four themes are procedural rather than mechanical, addressable by deployment policy (informed consent, anonymisation review, a scope statement on data use); the lecturer-bias concern is a real risk of any prioritisation tool and is the single argument in this survey for keeping the dashboard's outputs advisory rather than decisional.

\subsubsection{Change Requests and Triangulation with Quantitative Results}
Q11 asked for one thing the respondent would change about the dashboard. Two respondents gave substantive answers, producing two themes (Table~\ref{tab:eval-survey-themes}): cross-session aggregation (one response, asking for a view of how frequently a student is struggling across labs rather than within a single session), and criteria transparency (one response, asking for a definitive statement of what feeds into the score in terms of learning, response, and participation). The first is a future-work item that requires persisting per-snapshot data beyond the live session window (Chapter~\ref{sec:conclusion}); the second is a documentation gap that this thesis itself partially addresses through the design and implementation chapters, but which is not exposed in the deployed UI beyond the Settings page's weight listing.

The survey corroborates the quantitative results of Section~\ref{sec:eval-results} on three of the four §5.4 findings and adds one operational caveat. On identification (Q3) the 60\% "not easy" rate confirms that the problem the struggle leaderboard targets is a real one in the room rather than an artefact of the model framing; the deployed model's Spearman $\rho = +0.588$ against the four-band rater (Section~\ref{sec:eval-results}) sets a quantitative ceiling on how much of that gap the leaderboard can close. The operational caveat is the Q9 discomfort finding: 30\% of respondents would be uncomfortable being the scored student, even though the dashboard is instructor-facing and the student is not shown their own score. The lecturer-bias theme in Q10 makes the mechanism concrete: a struggler flagged repeatedly across weeks risks an implicit reputation effect even if the dashboard makes no automatic intervention. None of the four §5.4 findings speaks to this risk; addressing it is a policy and consent task rather than a model task, and is recorded as a deployment prerequisite in the limitations of Section~\ref{sec:eval-tradeoffs}.

\subsection{Discussion} \label{sec:eval-discussion}

\subsubsection{Difficulty Model and the IRT Anti-Result} \label{sec:eval-irt}

The 2PL IRT difficulty model is the only model evaluated in this chapter that does not improve on its hand-set baseline. Under the LLM rater's four-band difficulty rating, the IRT difficulty $b_q$ scaled to $[0, 1]$ achieves Spearman $\rho = +0.345$ against the rater, compared with $\rho = +0.468$ for the baseline composite (Figure~\ref{fig:eval-negative-findings}, left panel); the top-10 hardest set overlaps with the composite's by only one question out of ten (Figure~\ref{fig:eval-comparison-difficulty-scatter} and Table~\ref{tab:eval-comparison-difficulty-top10}). Linear-weighted band agreement is similarly weak: $\kappa = -0.016$ at the hand-set cutpoints and $\kappa = -0.024$ at the constrained-CV-trained cutpoints (Table~\ref{tab:eval-thresholds}), so the band threshold training pass made the agreement marginally worse rather than better and the IRT row was retained at its hand-set thresholds.

The reason for the divergence may be that the two models work from different inputs. The 2PL $b_q$ parameter is estimated only from the joint response matrix of attempts and correctness, recovering the latent ability level at which a student has a $50\%$ probability of answering the item correctly. The LLM rater, in contrast, is shown per-question signals and asked to map each question to one of the four explicit difficulty bands defined in the deployed dashboard's classification. The baseline composite uses the same aggregate signals under a fitted OLS weighting, which is why its $\rho = +0.468$ against the rater is closer than IRT's $\rho = +0.345$: both the composite and the rater work from the same underlying performance signals, while IRT works from a different decomposition of the response matrix. Both are valid measurements of `difficulty', but they answer different instructor questions, and the difficulty leaderboard surfaces only one of them at a time.

The deployment consequence is that the baseline composite is the difficulty default in \texttt{config.py}; the IRT difficulty toggle is retained but not promoted to being the deployed model. The 2PL fit itself remains valuable as a per-skill mastery signal (it feeds the BKT layer of the improved struggle model in Section~\ref{sec:eval-results}) and is reported as a model of response-pattern difficulty separately rather than as a competing pedagogical-difficulty scorer.

\subsubsection{Tradeoffs and Threats to Validity} \label{sec:eval-tradeoffs}

\paragraph{Rater fidelity}
Every trained model number reported in Sections~\ref{sec:eval-results} and~\ref{sec:eval-discussion} is measured against the LLM rater, so its strength is bounded by how well that rater itself agrees with a human judgement on the same data. On a fifty-snapshot stratified calibration set rated independently by the author, the LLM rater achieved linear-weighted Cohen's $\kappa = +0.198$ and quadratic-weighted $\kappa = +0.262$, with exact-band agreement of $38.0\%$ and within-one-band agreement of $68.0\%$. These are fair-to-moderate values rather than strong ones, so the deployed model's apparent agreement with the rater (Sections~\ref{sec:eval-results} and~\ref{sec:eval-thresholds}) should be read as a ceiling. A model that perfectly reproduced the rater's labels would still inherit the rater's $38\%$ exact disagreement with a human reader.

\paragraph{Model-versus-rater band agreement}
A second, distinct tradeoff is how often the deployed model and the LLM rater agree at the band level (rather than how often the rater agrees with a human). For the deployed struggle composite this is $47.3\%$ exact agreement and $71.7\%$ within-one-band against the rater, with linear-weighted $\kappa = +0.379$ (struggle) and $\kappa = +0.225$ (difficulty). Within-one-band agreement is the more honest metric for an ordinal scale because the difference between adjacent bands often reflects threshold placement rather than a meaningful disagreement about severity. Together with the rater-fidelity numbers above, this places the deployed system at a measured-but-imperfect agreement with both its training target and the human reader, which is consistent with the chapter's recommendation that the dashboard's outputs be treated as advisory.


\subsubsection{Limitations}

The first limitation is the module skew on which the threshold training was performed. Of the $1{,}306$ struggle snapshots, $47\%$ were rated Needs Help by the LLM and only $6\%$ On Track (Section~\ref{sec:eval-results}); of the $72$ unique questions, $72\%$ were rated Very Hard and none Easy. The constrained-CV cutpoints reported in Table~\ref{tab:eval-thresholds} reflect that distribution and should be re-trained rather than reused on a cohort with a different mix; the continuous score is more portable than the bands.

The second limitation is the small effective sample size for the difficulty model. With $n = 72$ questions, leave-one-out cross-validation is the chosen scheme due to the small sample size that preserves enough training data per fold to fit the five-signal regression, but it inflates the per-fold $\kappa$ variance and rules out bootstrap confidence intervals comparable to the struggle model's $1{,}306$-snapshot folds (Section~\ref{sec:eval-results}). The struggle results are statistically grounded, the difficulty results should be read as a directional finding which would benefit from a larger question pool.

The third limitation concerns the underlying incorrectness signal. The deployed OpenAI scorer returns its fallback midpoint value of $0.5$ for the majority of the $42{,}443$ submissions(Figure~\ref{fig:eval-incorrectness-distribution}), because most submissions lack the AI feedback the scorer reads. The later modelling stages is therefore working from a degraded primary signal on most rows, and the v2 weight refit corrects for what it can measure but cannot recover information that the scorer never produced. Fixing the scorer so it is to a non-fallback rate would be a higher leverage future improvement than retraining any of the weights or thresholds reported in this chapter.
