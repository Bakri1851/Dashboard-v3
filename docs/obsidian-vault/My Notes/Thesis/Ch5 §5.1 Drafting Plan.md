## Methodology decisions applied

- **Latent struggle is unobservable**, so it is operationalised as future observable behaviour over a horizon \(\Delta\). At time \(t\) the dashboard outputs \(S_t(s)\), \(D_t(q)\) from data in \([0, t]\); validated against outcomes in \([t, t+\Delta]\).
- **Default \(\Delta = 15\) minutes** — chosen to match the typical interval between two consecutive instructor scans of a lab, and to keep the labelling window within a single session.
- **Three converging strands**:
  1. **Direct predictive** — IRT and BKT against held-out next-attempt correctness; metrics AUC + log-loss + Brier; baselines majority-class, per-question mean, per-student mean.
  2. **Horizon-shifted** — composite \(S_t(s)\) and \(D_t(q)\) against the three labels below; metrics Spearman \(\rho\), top-\(k\) P/R, NDCG@\(k\), AUC.
  3. **Structural** — drop-one ablation on the 7 struggle signals; \(\pm 20\%\) weight perturbation; V1/V2 Spearman convergence on replayed sessions.
- **Three labels** (all self-derived from submission data; no external annotation):
  - \(L_{\text{top20}}(s, t, \Delta) = 1\) iff \(s\) is in the top-20 by mean incorrectness in \([t, t+\Delta]\)
  - \(L_{\text{needs\_help}}(s, t, \Delta) = 1\) iff \(s\) exhibits a retry spiral (\(\geq 3\) near-duplicate wrong submissions) OR abandonment (inter-submission gap \(> 3\times\) personal baseline)
  - \(L_{\text{struggling\_plus}} = L_{\text{top20}} \vee L_{\text{needs\_help}}\)
- **Self-derived label justification** — anonymised IDs ruled out grade linkage; deployment scope ruled out instructor flagging and expert annotation. Frame as a deliberate methodological choice, not a missing dataset.

```latex
This chapter evaluates the dashboard system of Chapter~\ref{sec:implementation} along two dimensions, the validity of its model outputs --- by which we mean whether the struggle and difficulty rankings it surfaces agree with observable student behaviour --- and the operational behaviour of the deployed software against the responsiveness, interpretability, and robustness targets implied in Chapter~\ref{sec:design}. The unit of evaluation is the individual weekly lab session as the dashboard is designed to be consulted live within a single session, and the cohort consists of around sixty students across approximately twelve weekly sessions, with each student producing between roughly five and one hundred submissions in a given window depending on engagement.

The evaluation pursues three converging strands. The first is a direct predictive strand that assesses the probabilistic models, IRT and BKT, against the truly observable signal of next-attempt correctness on held-out submissions. The second is a horizon-shifted strand that evaluates the composite struggle and difficulty scores against labels constructed from observable behaviour over a fixed horizon \(\Delta\) after the cutoff. The third is a structural strand that examines the stability of the resulting rankings under signal ablation and weight perturbation, and the convergence of the V1 and V2 implementations on the same replayed sessions. A supplementary survey strand (Section~\ref{sec:eval-survey}) supplies user-perception evidence on interpretability, trust, and student comfort.

This chapter is organised as follows. Section~\ref{sec:eval-design} sets out the scope, evidence sources, retrospective protocol, and limitations of the evaluation methodology. Sections~\ref{sec:eval-functional} and \ref{sec:eval-nonfunctional} present the functional and non-functional verification of the deployed software. Section~\ref{sec:eval-results} presents the quantitative model-comparison results. Section~\ref{sec:eval-survey} reports the survey findings. Section~\ref{sec:eval-discussion} integrates the strands and reflects on tradeoffs, threats to validity, and limitations.
```

### §5.1.1 Scope of Evaluation

Tightened after preamble extraction to focus on the in/out-of-scope choices and their rationale.

```latex
The evaluation has two tracks. The first is the v2 weight-refit programme of Section~\ref{sec:eval-v2-methodology}, which fits the deployed composite weights of Chapter~\ref{sec:design} against second-opinion labels obtained from a large language model rater and assesses the resulting v2 vectors against the v1 baseline under session-grouped cross-validation; the refit's empirical results land in Sections~\ref{sec:eval-compare-struggle} through~\ref{sec:eval-findings}, and its rater-fidelity check is reported in Section~\ref{sec:eval-compare-struggle}. The second is a structured functional and non-functional verification of the deployed V2 stack against the requirements of Chapter~\ref{sec:design}, with the body of Sections~\ref{sec:eval-functional} and~\ref{sec:eval-nonfunctional} carrying the qualitative summary and the full row-by-row checklists landing in Appendix~\ref{app:detailed-test-results}. A behaviour-based replay protocol against self-derived labels was considered as an alternative ranking-validity check but was incompatible with the available cohort size and the deployment-window length; it is left as future work and re-flagged in Section~\ref{sec:eval-discussion}.

A prospective intervention study, which would test whether instructor decisions actually informed by the dashboard improve student outcomes, is explicitly out of scope as the deployment window did not permit a controlled comparison and the absence of grade linkage or instructor annotation on the available data would have made any resulting causal claim unsupportable in any case. We do not run a load test, a security audit, or an accessibility audit; the operational evidence we report concerns the dashboard's behaviour in a typical classroom-scale single-session use and not its behaviour at the boundaries of its design envelope.
```

### §5.1.2 Evidence Sources

```latex
The evaluation draws on five evidence sources. The first is the deployment submission log, a newline-delimited JSON record exported from the platform's submission API and parsed by the data loader of Section~\ref{sec:analytics} into a normalised DataFrame indexed by student, question, and timestamp. The log spans the academic period of the deployment (2026-02-02 to 2026-05-15) and contains the per-submission fields the model pipeline relies on, namely the question identifier, module label, submitted answer text, instructor feedback string, and timestamp, from which all derived signals (attempt counts, retry distances, inter-submission gaps, incorrectness scores) are recomputed at replay time; the v2 refit programme draws on 1{,}306 stratified snapshots and seventy-two unique questions derived from this log.

The second source is the second-opinion label set obtained from a large language model rater (GPT-4o-mini), which assigns each of the 1{,}306 stratified snapshots a four-band severity rating and each of the seventy-two unique questions a four-band difficulty rating. The label set underpins the v2 weight-refit programme of Sections~\ref{sec:eval-v2-methodology} and \ref{sec:eval-findings}. Its inter-rater fidelity against a fifty-snapshot author calibration set is reported in Section~\ref{sec:eval-compare-struggle} and bounds the strength of the v2 numbers.

The third source is the survey of Section~\ref{sec:eval-survey}, an eleven-question Microsoft Forms instrument distributed to students and staff in the deployment cohort, which provides Likert and free-text evidence on baseline lab support, dashboard interpretability, trust, and ethical concerns. The instrument is reproduced in full in Appendix~\ref{app:detailed-test-results}.

The fourth source is a structured functional smoke-test sequence executed on the V2 deployment, in which each functional requirement from Chapter~\ref{sec:design} is exercised manually and the observed behaviour recorded; the resulting checklist appears in Appendix~\ref{app:detailed-test-results}, and the body of this chapter (Section~\ref{sec:eval-functional}) records only the qualitative summary and the small number of items that failed or required workaround.

The fifth source is the V2 prewarm instrumentation log, which records the wall-clock cost of each startup phase (incorrectness cache warm, IRT fit, BKT fit, struggle leaderboard ready, difficulty leaderboard ready) together with per-skill BKT predictive AUC, and which underpins the latency and per-skill BKT evidence reported in Section~\ref{sec:eval-results}.
```

### §5.1.4 Limitations of Evaluation

```latex
Several limitations bound the strength of the claims that follow, all of which follow from the deployment context rather than from oversight in the protocol design. The first is the small effective cohort, with the v2 weight-refit programme of Section~\ref{sec:eval-v2-methodology} drawing on 1{,}306 stratified snapshots across twenty-one healthy sessions for the struggle composite and the improved-blend, and seventy-two unique questions for the difficulty composite; the per-session counts are sufficient for the rank-quality metrics but limit the statistical power available for any inferential claim, and the bootstrap confidence intervals reported alongside Spearman \(\rho\) and top-\(k\) overlap should be read in that light.

The second is the single-cohort, single-instructor, single-institution character of the deployment, which bounds external validity; the dashboard's ranking behaviour on a different cohort, with a different module curriculum or a different submission cadence, is not directly inferable from the results presented here, and we report findings as evidence from one deployment rather than as general statements about the dashboard's performance class.

The third is the retrospective nature of the evaluation, which is necessary as discussed above but which means the chapter says nothing about whether instructor decisions actually informed by the dashboard improve student outcomes; the v2 refit programme reports rank agreement with the LLM rater, not causal impact on student outcomes, and any causal interpretation is left to a prospective study outside the scope of this submission.
```
