## Methodology decisions applied

- **Latent struggle is unobservable**, so it is operationalised as future observable behaviour over a horizon \(\Delta\). At time \(t\) the dashboard outputs \(S_t(s)\), \(D_t(q)\) from data in \([0, t]\); validated against outcomes in \([t, t+\Delta]\).
- **Default \(\Delta = 15\) minutes** — chosen to match the typical interval between two consecutive instructor scans of a lab, and to keep the labelling window within a single session.
- **Three converging strands**:
  1. **Direct predictive** — IRT and BKT against held-out next-attempt correctness; metrics AUC + log-loss + Brier; baselines majority-class, per-question mean, per-student mean.
  2. **Horizon-shifted** — composite \(S_t(s)\) and \(D_t(q)\) against the three labels below; metrics Spearman \(\rho\), top-\(k\) P/R, NDCG@\(k\), AUC.
  3. **Structural** — drop-one ablation on the 7 struggle signals; \(\pm 20\%\) weight perturbation; V1/V2 Spearman convergence on replayed sessions.
- **Three labels** (all self-derived from submission data; no external annotation):
  - \(L_{\text{top20}}(s, t, \Delta) = 1\) iff \(s\) is in the top-20 by mean incorrectness in \([t, t+\Delta]\)
  - \(L_{\text{needs\_help}}(s, t, \Delta) = 1\) iff \(s\) exhibits a retry spiral (\(\geq 3\) near-duplicate wrong submissions) OR abandonment (inter-submission gap \(> 3\times\) personal baseline)
  - \(L_{\text{struggling\_plus}} = L_{\text{top20}} \vee L_{\text{needs\_help}}\)
- **Self-derived label justification** — anonymised IDs ruled out grade linkage; deployment scope ruled out instructor flagging and expert annotation. Frame as a deliberate methodological choice, not a missing dataset.

### §5.1.1 Scope of Evaluation

Tightened after preamble extraction to focus on the in/out-of-scope choices and their rationale.

```latex
The evaluation is deliberately retrospective; we replay submission logs already collected during the deployment and assess each model output at varying cutoff times \(T\) against ground-truth labels derived from later behaviour in the same logs, which lets us examine the dashboard's prioritisation as if it were running live without conducting a fresh classroom experiment. A prospective intervention study, which would test whether instructor decisions informed by the dashboard improve student outcomes, is explicitly out of scope as the deployment window did not permit a controlled comparison and the absence of grade linkage or instructor annotation on the available data would have made any resulting causal claim unsupportable in any case.

The functional verification of Section~\ref{sec:eval-functional} is similarly bounded; we walk through each functional requirement from Chapter~\ref{sec:design} as a smoke-test of the deployed V2 stack, recording observed behaviour against expected behaviour, and present only the qualitative summary in the body with the full row-by-row checklist appearing in Appendix~\ref{app:detailed-test-results}. We do not run a load test, a security audit, or an accessibility audit; the operational evidence we report concerns the dashboard's behaviour in a typical classroom-scale single-session use and not its behaviour at the boundaries of its design envelope.
```

### §5.1.2 Evidence Sources

```latex
The evaluation draws on four evidence sources. The primary source is the deployment submission log, a newline-delimited JSON record exported from the platform's submission API and parsed by the data loader of Section~\ref{sec:analytics} into a normalised DataFrame indexed by student, question, and timestamp. The log spans the academic period of the deployment and contains the per-submission fields the model pipeline relies on, namely the question identifier, module label, submitted answer text, instructor feedback string, and timestamp, from which all derived signals (attempt counts, retry distances, inter-submission gaps, incorrectness scores) are recomputed at replay time.

The second source is the survey of Section~\ref{sec:eval-survey}, an eleven-question Microsoft Forms instrument distributed to students and staff in the deployment cohort, which provides Likert and free-text evidence on baseline lab support, dashboard interpretability, trust, and ethical concerns. The instrument is reproduced in full in Appendix~\ref{app:detailed-test-results}.

The third source is a structured functional smoke-test sequence executed on the V2 deployment, in which each functional requirement from Chapter~\ref{sec:design} is exercised manually and the observed behaviour recorded; the resulting checklist appears in Appendix~\ref{app:detailed-test-results}, and the body of this chapter (Section~\ref{sec:eval-functional}) records only the qualitative summary and the small number of items that failed or required workaround.

The fourth source is the V2 prewarm instrumentation log, which records the wall-clock cost of each startup phase (incorrectness cache warm, IRT fit, BKT fit, struggle leaderboard ready, difficulty leaderboard ready) together with per-skill BKT predictive AUC, and which underpins the latency and per-skill BKT evidence reported in Section~\ref{sec:eval-results}.
```

### §5.1.3 Retrospective Evaluation Protocol

```latex
Latent student struggle is, by construction, unobservable. The dashboard cannot consult an oracle that knows which students are genuinely struggling at time \(t\); the closest available proxy is to operationalise struggle as future observable behaviour over a fixed horizon \(\Delta\) and to ask whether the dashboard's score at \(t\), computed from data in \([0, t]\), agrees with that future behaviour in \([t, t+\Delta]\). Throughout this chapter the default horizon is \(\Delta = 15\) minutes, which matches the typical interval between an instructor's two consecutive scans of a lab and which keeps the labelling window short enough to retain a within-session interpretation.

Three labels are derived from this construction, all of them computed entirely from submission data and requiring no external annotation:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \(L_{\text{top20}}(s, t, \Delta) = 1\) iff student \(s\) is among the top twenty students by mean incorrectness over the submissions falling in \([t, t+\Delta]\);
  \item \(L_{\text{needs\_help}}(s, t, \Delta) = 1\) iff \(s\) exhibits in \([t, t+\Delta]\) either a \emph{retry spiral}, defined as three or more near-duplicate wrong submissions on the same question, or an \emph{abandonment event}, defined as an inter-submission gap exceeding three times the student's personal baseline gap;
  \item \(L_{\text{struggling\_plus}}(s, t, \Delta) = L_{\text{top20}}(s, t, \Delta) \vee L_{\text{needs\_help}}(s, t, \Delta)\).
\end{itemize}

The deliberate use of self-derived labels follows from the deployment constraints. Student identifiers in the submission log are anonymised, which rules out grade linkage; the deployment scope did not include instructor flagging or expert annotation of struggle episodes; and any externally-imposed label would itself require independent validation before use as ground truth. Framing the labels as observable future behaviour over a fixed horizon makes the construction transparent, falsifiable in principle (a later study could substitute instructor flags and re-run the same protocol), and faithful to the operational decision the dashboard is supposed to inform, which is whether to direct attention to a particular student now on the basis of their likely behaviour shortly afterwards.

Against this scaffolding the three evaluation strands instantiate as follows. The direct predictive strand evaluates the probabilistic models on held-out next-attempt correctness, with each model trained on data up to cutoff \(T\) and tested on the immediately subsequent attempts of the held-out fold, reporting predictive AUC, log-loss, and Brier score against three reference baselines (majority-class, per-question mean correctness, per-student mean correctness). The horizon-shifted strand evaluates the composite struggle score \(S_t(s)\) and the question difficulty score \(D_t(q)\) against the three labels above, using AUC together with rank-quality metrics (Spearman \(\rho\), top-\(k\) precision and recall, NDCG@\(k\)) at a schedule of cutoffs \(T\) spanning the session. The structural strand applies a drop-one ablation on the seven weighted signals of the baseline struggle model and a \(\pm 20\%\) weight perturbation sensitivity check, both reporting Spearman rank stability against the nominal ranking, and a V1-versus-V2 Spearman convergence check on replayed sessions to confirm the two implementations rank consistently.
```

### §5.1.4 Limitations of Evaluation

```latex
Several limitations bound the strength of the claims that follow, all of which follow from the deployment context rather than from oversight in the protocol design. The first is the small effective cohort, with around sixty students across approximately twelve weekly sessions; the per-session counts are sufficient for the rank-quality metrics but limit the statistical power available for any inferential claim, and the bootstrap confidence intervals reported alongside Spearman \(\rho\) and top-\(k\) overlap should be read in that light.

The second is the single-cohort, single-instructor, single-institution character of the deployment, which bounds external validity; the dashboard's ranking behaviour on a different cohort, with a different module curriculum or a different submission cadence, is not directly inferable from the results presented here, and we report findings as evidence from one deployment rather than as general statements about the dashboard's performance class.

The third is the retrospective nature of the protocol, which is necessary as discussed above but which means the chapter says nothing about whether instructor decisions actually informed by the dashboard improve student outcomes; the labels operationalise struggle as future observable behaviour over the horizon \(\Delta\), they do not claim to capture whether a student would have benefited from intervention, and any causal interpretation is left to a prospective study outside the scope of this submission.

The fourth is the self-derived character of the ground-truth labels themselves. The construction is transparent and the protocol is reproducible; nonetheless, the labels are computed from the same submission stream that the models are scored on, which means that the temporal split between training and labelling windows (\([0, t]\) versus \([t, t+\Delta]\)) carries the full weight of the independence claim, and any failure of the temporal split would propagate into both the model and the label simultaneously.
```
