### §4.9.0 — Preamble (paste at line 1087)

```latex
The dashboard's instructor functionality is implemented twice; the V1 implementation lives in \texttt{code/learning\_dashboard/ui/views.py} as the seven Streamlit page functions serving port 8501, whilst V2 lives in \texttt{code2/frontend/src/views/} as eight React views served by the same FastAPI process on port 8000. Parity is deliberate as V2 reimplements every V1 view to make the apples-to-apples evaluation in Chapter \ref{sec:evaluation} possible, but the second version also adds one view with no V1 equivalent --- the Session Progression view --- which replays an ended session through evenly-sized time buckets to support retrospective debriefs. ### §4.9.1 — In-Class View (paste at line 1098)
```

```latex
The In-Class view is the canonical live monitoring surface and is what the instructor sees first on launch. Both versions present two side-by-side leaderboards --- top strugglers by descending struggle score, top hardest questions by descending difficulty --- each capped at \texttt{LEADERBOARD\_MAX\_ITEMS = 15}, with bar colours following the threshold bands from Section \ref{sec:design} (green for \emph{On Track}, amber for \emph{Minor Issues}, orange for \emph{Struggling} and red for \emph{Needs Help}; the difficulty leaderboard reuses the same colour ramp with \emph{Easy}, \emph{Medium}, \emph{Hard} and \emph{Very Hard} labels). Beneath the leaderboards sits the collaborative filtering diagnostic panel from Section \ref{sec:analytics}, which lists the peers similar to flagged strugglers whose own parametric scores have not yet crossed the threshold, surfacing students whose behaviour pattern matches a known struggler before their score does. Two distribution histograms (struggle scores and difficulty scores, each rendered with \texttt{HISTOGRAM\_BINS = 20}) close the view, sharing the live cohort context with the leaderboards rather than living on a separate analytics page. Clicking a bar drills into the corresponding Student Detail or Question Detail view; V1 uses Streamlit's \texttt{st.plotly\_chart(on\_select="rerun")} with a \texttt{point\_index} lookup whilst V2 routes via React Router to \texttt{/student/:id} or \texttt{/question/:id}.

V2 carries a second presentation of the In-Class view, the Basic layout, which is the default the instructor sees on first load due to the \texttt{useUiPrefsStore} preference store initialising the \texttt{inClassViewMode} value to \texttt{'basic'}; the layout is selectable through the Settings page's \emph{In-class layout} toggle. The Basic layout collapses each leaderboard into a single colour-coded bar per student or question, stretched to fill the row using a cohort min-max scale with a minimum visible fill so the lowest score is still a visible nub, and pairs the two leaderboards with three small panels (struggle distribution, difficulty distribution, last-24-hour submissions). Switching to Advanced restores the full Plotly leaderboards, the CF diagnostic panel and the score-distribution histograms described above, and also widens the Student Detail and Question Detail views into two-column layouts with the additional model-evidence panels described later in this section, making the Basic/Advanced choice a global density preference rather than a per-view setting. V1 has no equivalent toggle as the Streamlit layout is fixed.
```

### §4.9.2 — Student Detail View (paste at line 1102)

```latex
The Student Detail view renders the per-signal breakdown of a single student's struggle score, with one bar per of the seven baseline signals defined in Section \ref{sec:analytics} ($N$ submission count, $T$ time per attempt, $I$ recent incorrectness, $R$ retry rate, $A$ activity intensity, $D$ improvement slope and $\mathrm{Rep}$ repeat-question rate), alongside a submission timeline ordered by timestamp with incorrectness encoded as the bar colour and the top \texttt{STUDENT\_DETAIL\_TOP\_QUESTIONS = 10} hardest questions for this student, each row of which drills into the corresponding Question Detail page. When the Improved Models toggle in Settings is enabled, the view adds a model-evidence panel sourced from Section \ref{sec:advanced} --- the per-skill BKT mastery values for the modules the student has touched (using the four parameters $P(L_0)$, $P(T)$, $P(G)$ and $P(S)$ from Section \ref{sec:design}, with the \texttt{BKT\_MASTERY\_THRESHOLD = 0.95} bar marking the ``mastered'' badge) together with the improved struggle decomposition into its behavioural, mastery-gap and IRT-difficulty-adjusted components, which lets the instructor see why the improved score differs from the baseline rather than just that it does.
```

### §4.9.3 — Question Detail View (paste at line 1106)

```latex
The Question Detail view shows the question text alongside its difficulty score and aggregated incorrectness, with the measurement-confidence indicator from Section \ref{sec:advanced} rendered as a coloured chip beside the incorrectness value (green when $\kappa$ exceeds the high threshold, amber for intermediate values and grey when $\kappa$ falls below the low threshold), so the instructor can tell at a glance whether the LLM verdict is trustworthy enough to act on. The top \texttt{QUESTION\_DETAIL\_TOP\_STUDENTS = 15} students who struggled on this question are listed beneath the header sorted by per-question incorrectness rather than by overall struggle, as the relevant ranking here is who failed on this specific item rather than who is generally finding the lab hard. Below the student list the mistake clusters from Section \ref{sec:analytics} are surfaced as a row of cards, each carrying its OpenAI-labelled centroid, the member count and up to \texttt{CLUSTER\_MAX\_EXAMPLES = 3} representative answers truncated to \texttt{CLUSTER\_EXAMPLE\_MAX\_CHARS = 300} characters for the UI. The RAG per-question suggestions panel from Section \ref{sec:rag} closes the view, returning two to three short coaching bullets tailored to the dominant misconception patterns rather than to any individual student, which gives the instructor a class-wide intervention point rather than a per-student one.
```

### §4.9.4 — Data Analysis View (paste at line 1110)

```latex
The Data Analysis view exposes cohort-level summaries across the academic-week window selected in the sidebar, using the \texttt{academic\_calendar} module to map raw calendar dates into the Loughborough 2025/26 week labels (induction week, weeks 1--12, winter break, weeks 13--24) so that instructor questions phrased in academic time can be answered without manual date arithmetic. A selectbox switches between six chart modes --- module usage, top questions, user activity, activity timeline, activity by academic week and students by module --- with the top-questions and user-activity tables capped at \texttt{DATA\_ANALYSIS\_TOP\_QUESTIONS = 10} and \texttt{DATA\_ANALYSIS\_TOP\_USERS = 20} respectively, and each row in the latter two tables links straight to the corresponding detail page. The activity-by-week chart in V2 is rendered as a week-by-day heatmap rather than V1's bar chart, which exposes the daily rhythm within an academic week in addition to the week-over-week trend; the underlying \texttt{academic\_calendar} mapping is shared between the two versions so the period semantics are identical.
```

### §4.9.5 — Model Comparison View (paste at line 1114)

```latex
The Model Comparison view sits behind the Improved Models toggle and feeds the quantitative evaluation in Chapter \ref{sec:evaluation} directly. Both versions split into two tabs: the Students tab plots baseline struggle from Section \ref{sec:analytics} against the improved struggle from Section \ref{sec:advanced} on a scatter chart colour-coded by level-agreement --- same band, one band apart, two bands apart or three bands apart --- and surfaces headline metrics including Spearman $\rho$, the top-10 overlap proportion and the level-agreement breakdown, whilst the Questions tab does the same for the baseline difficulty against the IRT difficulty $\sigma(\hat{\beta}_q)$ from Section \ref{sec:advanced}. Each tab also carries a ``largest mover'' table beside the scatter --- the student or question whose band changed most under the improved model --- drawing attention to the cases where the model choice actually matters for triage rather than just for the headline correlations. The view is the comparison-study artefact promised by Section \ref{sec:design} and is the primary visual input to the Chapter \ref{sec:evaluation} evaluation.
```

### §4.9.6 — Settings View (paste at line 1118)

```latex
Both versions expose the runtime model configuration through a Settings page. The toggles common to both versions are Sound Effects (\texttt{SOUNDS\_ENABLED\_DEFAULT = True}), Auto-Refresh (\texttt{AUTO\_REFRESH\_DEFAULT = True}) with the refresh interval selectable from \texttt{AUTO\_REFRESH\_OPTIONS = [5, 10, 15, 30, 60, 120, 300]} seconds, Collaborative Filtering (\texttt{cf\_enabled}) with the similarity-threshold slider $\tau$, RAG suggestions (\texttt{RAG\_ENABLED\_DEFAULT = True}), and the two model-choice selectboxes --- Struggle Model (Baseline / Improved) and Difficulty Model (Baseline / IRT) --- which together drive the canonical \texttt{improved\_models\_enabled} flag in V1 session state and the \texttt{runtime\_config.RuntimeConfig} record in V2. When Improved is selected, four sliders appear for the BKT parameters $P(L_0)$, $P(T)$, $P(G)$ and $P(S)$ with defaults from Section \ref{sec:advanced}; a slider change re-triggers the per-student mastery computation on the next refresh. The two versions diverge in a few places: V1 exposes Temporal Smoothing (\texttt{SMOOTHING\_ENABLED}) as a runtime toggle whilst V2 hard-codes the EMA at $\alpha = 0.3$ and only surfaces \texttt{smoothing\_alpha} in a read-only configuration reference at the bottom of the page; V2 adds a theme picker offering seven visual skins (sci-fi neon, blueprint, matrix, cyberpunk, paper, slate and zen) that persist to \texttt{localStorage}; and V2 carries the In-Class layout toggle (Basic / Advanced) described in Section \ref{sec:views} above.

V1 carries a small flourish that V2 does not: six short sci-fi cues fired at session start, session end, student selection, data refresh and other key events, authored as Web Audio API \texttt{OscillatorNode} and \texttt{GainNode} chains in \texttt{code/learning\_dashboard/sound.py} and injected through a zero-height iframe via \texttt{st.components.v1.html()} --- as Streamlit cannot natively play audio, the zero-height iframe is the standard pattern for embedding browser-side JavaScript without affecting the page layout. The cues are gated by the Sound Effects toggle and, while not load-bearing for the analytical contribution, are the kind of UX detail that helps the instructor track session state at a glance without staring at the screen.
```

### §4.9.7 — Previous Sessions View (paste at line 1122)

```latex
The Previous Sessions view lists every saved session in descending start-time order --- name, module, start and end timestamps, computed duration, and a per-row delete button --- reading from \texttt{data/saved\_sessions.json} as described in Section \ref{sec:session}. Sessions can be filtered by academic period using the same \texttt{academic\_calendar} module that drives the Data Analysis view, which keeps the list manageable across a full year of teaching. Clicking a row applies the saved session's time window via the deferred-actions pattern described in Section \ref{sec:structure} --- V1 writes \texttt{pending\_session\_load\_record} into session state so that the next rerun rehydrates the filter state before the sidebar widgets render, whilst V2 mutates the client-side filter store directly through the React state hooks; in both cases the page then routes to In-Class so that the instructor can re-view the historical cohort at the moment that session ended.
```

### §4.9.8 — Session Progression View (V2-only) (paste under the new heading)

```latex
The Session Progression view is V2-only and lives at \texttt{code2/frontend/src/views/SessionProgression.tsx}. It replays an ended session as twelve evenly-sized time buckets by default --- configurable from three to sixty through the \texttt{buckets} query parameter on \texttt{/api/sessions/\{id\}/progression}, although the React view does not expose this control to the instructor --- and animates a stacked-area chart of the four struggle bands through the timeline, with a per-bucket snapshot panel listing the cumulative submission count, the unique student count, the mean incorrectness and the identifiers of students flagged as \emph{Needs Help} at that point in time. The view is useful for retrospective debriefs as it answers questions of the form \emph{when in the lab did this student start to deteriorate?} or \emph{when did this question start attracting wrong answers?}, which the live In-Class leaderboards cannot answer due to displaying only the final aggregate state. V1 has no equivalent as the bucket animation would require either rerunning the Streamlit page at a sub-second cadence (not viable due to the per-rerun cost of the analytics pipeline) or significant manual re-architecture into a callback-driven layer; V2 handles it natively in React due to the layout and animation being client-side and the per-bucket analytics being cached on disk in \texttt{data/progression\_cache/} for instant warm replays.
```
