## Insertion 5 — Ch2 §2.2.4 "Empirical Refinement of Composite Models" (~170 words)

**Anchor**: under the §2.2.4 stub header inserted between [`requirements-specification.tex:137 and 139`](../../../../Report/main-sections/requirements-specification.tex#L137).

```latex
Supervised refinement of composite weights --- typically by logistic regression or gradient boosting trained against externally-collected labels of student state --- is a methodological alternative to the hand-set or manually-tuned weighting schemes used in many of the composite-scoring works reviewed above. The supervisory signal need not be human-annotated: recent work on large language models as second-opinion raters demonstrates substantial agreement with human annotators on classification tasks at a small fraction of the cost \cite{gilardiChatGPTOutperformsCrowd2023, chiangCanLargeLanguage2023, zhengJudgingLLMasaJudgeMTBench2023}, with inter-rater agreement quantified through Cohen's $\kappa$ \cite{CoefficientAgreementNominal} and interpreted against the bands set out by Landis and Koch \cite{landisMeasurementObserverAgreement1977}. Where the weight space is small but contains scalar hyperparameters tuned by trial and error, modern Bayesian optimisation libraries such as Optuna's Tree-structured Parzen Estimator \cite{NIPS2011_86e8f7ab, akibaOptunaNextgenerationHyperparameter2019} produce stable optima in fewer trials than grid or random search. The empirical-refinement work reported in Chapter~\ref{sec:eval-results} adopts all three of these techniques to re-fit the v1 composite weights.
```

```latex
The deployed v1 composite weights of Sections~\ref{sec: student struggle} and~\ref{sec: task difficulty} were tuned by trial and error in the absence of labelled ground-truth data, as noted above. To close that loop empirically without re-instrumenting the deployed system, the design adopts a self-contained offline pipeline that re-fits the composite weights against second-opinion labels obtained from a large language model rater (GPT-4o-mini), evaluates the refined v2 vectors against the same retrospective protocol the deployed v1 system is evaluated under, and exposes the result as a runtime toggle so the v1 vectors remain the deployed default.

The pipeline is structured in five sequential phases with frozen on-disk hand-offs between them, as Figure~\ref{fig:v2-pipeline-design} shows: a one-shot data fetch from the submission API, a stratified snapshot sampler, an LLM-rater labelling stage that also collects a fifty-snapshot author calibration to support an inter-rater agreement check, a fitting stage that produces three composite weight vectors and two scalar hyperparameters, and a synthesis notebook that generates the figures and tables of Chapter~\ref{sec:eval-results}. The operational implementation of each phase is detailed in Section~\ref{sec:v2-training}.

Two design decisions are worth flagging in advance of Chapter~\ref{sec:implementation}. First, every phase outputs a frozen on-disk artefact (Parquet for raw data, JSON for snapshots, labels, weights, and hyperparameters), so the pipeline is restart-safe at any phase boundary and any downstream phase can be re-run without recomputing what precedes it. Second, the trained v2 vectors are deployment-ready: the runtime toggle in the V2 stack reads the same JSON files that Phase~4 writes, with no schema translation step between training output and runtime input.
```

## §5.4.9 Hyperparameter optimisation (~290 words; expanded 2026-05-25 with joint-study figures)

**Anchor**: under [`results-and-evaluation.tex` §5.4.9](../../../../Report/main-sections/results-and-evaluation.tex) `\label{sec:eval-hyperopt}`. Updated 2026-05-25 to drop the previous 4-panel `hyperparams_optuna.png` figure (two independent search landscapes + two TPE trajectories) in favour of the cleaner joint-study pair: the contour shows the K × τ optimisation surface in one panel, and the importance bar chart quantifies "$\tau$ matters, $K$ doesn't" as a number rather than a verbal claim.

```latex
Two scalar hyperparameters were tuned using Optuna's Tree-structured Parzen Estimator \cite{NIPS2011_86e8f7ab, akibaOptunaNextgenerationHyperparameter2019}, maximising session-grouped five-fold cross-validation Spearman~$\rho$ against the LLM's four-band rating. The Bayesian shrinkage constant $K$ was tuned in a 50-trial study over integers in $[1, 20]$, and the collaborative-filtering similarity threshold $\tau$ in a separate 50-trial study over reals in $[0.4, 0.9]$. TPE was preferred over grid or random search as the two search spaces are small enough for Bayesian methods to converge in tens of trials where a uniform sweep would require an order of magnitude more. The shrinkage constant $K$ moves from v1 default $5$ to v2 best $1$, with CV $\rho$ changing from $+0.431$ to $+0.444$ --- $\Delta\rho \approx +0.013$, within the per-fold standard deviation, so the result is a robustness validation of the hand-set value rather than an empirical refinement. The threshold $\tau$ moves from v1 default $0.7$ to v2 best $0.899$, with CV $\rho$ moving from $+0.234$ to $+0.434$ --- $\Delta\rho \approx +0.200$, a substantial improvement indicating that the v1 default was too permissive. The best $\tau$ sits at the upper boundary of the $[0.4, 0.9]$ search range, so a finer search may yield further improvement; this is noted as future work in Section~\ref{sec:eval-discussion}.

To validate the independence assumption that justified the two separate studies, a single joint $K \times \tau$ study was run over 100 trials with the joint objective $0.5\,(\rho_K + \rho_\tau)$. The joint optimum lands at $K = 1$ and $\tau = 0.898$, coinciding with the independent optima to within the third decimal place; this confirms that the two hyperparameters are effectively decoupled under the present objective. Figure~\ref{fig:eval-hyperparams-contour} shows the joint optimisation surface as a contour plot, with the high-$\rho$ ridge tracking $\tau$ near the upper boundary and the surface essentially flat along the $K$ axis. Figure~\ref{fig:eval-hyperparams-importances} quantifies this directly as a parameter-importance bar chart: $\tau$ accounts for the bulk of the joint-objective variance whereas $K$ contributes negligibly. The four BKT priors and the BKT mastery threshold were originally on the grid but were dropped from the v2 study as each trial would have required around thirty minutes of BKT refits; they remain at \texttt{config.py} defaults. Table~\ref{tab:eval-hyperparams} reports the headline numbers.
```

## §5.4.4 Per-Module Normalisation Effect (~280 words)

**Anchor**: existing `% TODO: 5.4.4 prose block` marker (was 5.4.5 pre-trim; renumbered after deletion of the BKT-AUC subsubsection).

```latex
The per-module normalisation introduced in V2 was a response to a Chapter~\ref{sec:problems} concern that V1's global min-max normalisation of the difficulty signals let a single high-difficulty module dominate the leaderboard rankings, suppressing per-module variation across the rest of the cohort. The V2 difficulty pipeline routes each raw signal through \texttt{min\_max\_normalise\_grouped} keyed by module; the raw and per-module-normalised columns are both preserved in the difficulty DataFrame, so the effect can be assessed retrospectively without re-running the pipeline.

Figure~\ref{fig:eval-permodule-normalisation} shows the per-module raw difficulty distributions side-by-side with the per-module-normalised distributions across the modules in the deployment cohort. Under the V1 global-normalised scheme, two modules dominate the top quintile and the rest of the cohort collapses into a near-flat distribution at the lower end; under the V2 per-module-normalised scheme, each module has its own internal spread and the leaderboard reflects within-module relative difficulty rather than cross-module absolute difficulty.

The effect is qualitative rather than quantitative for this evaluation --- there is no held-out ground truth for ``which questions are hard within their module'' against which to measure rank quality independently of the LLM rater's four-band rating used in Section~\ref{sec:eval-findings}. The intent is to motivate the design choice with the empirical distribution shapes; the quantitative claim is that per-module normalisation restored within-module signal that was lost under V1's global normalisation, which is what Chapter~\ref{sec:problems} originally posited.
```
